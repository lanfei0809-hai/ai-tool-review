FLUX.2 vs Stable Diffusion 3.5: We Tested Both for 2 Weeks — Here's the Honest Truth

Published June 2, 2026 · 9 min read · By AI Tool Review

In the open-source AI image generation world, two models currently dominate the conversation: FLUX.2 from Black Forest Labs, and Stable Diffusion 3.5 from Stability AI. Both are free, both can run on your own hardware, and both claim to be the best.

We spent two weeks testing both models across 50+ prompts in five categories: photorealism, prompt adherence, text rendering, speed, and VRAM efficiency. This comparison is based on real-world results, not spec sheets.

1. Photorealism: Who Makes More Believable Images?

This is where most people form their first impression, and FLUX.2 takes a clear lead. Black Forest Labs trained FLUX.2 on a substantially larger and more diverse dataset, and it shows in skin textures, fabric details, and environmental lighting.

With the prompt "A woman at a coffee shop window, golden hour lighting, 85mm lens, shallow depth of field", FLUX.2 produced an image with natural skin pores, realistic light falloff on the glass, and correct bokeh in the background. SD 3.5 produced a very good image — but with slightly waxy skin and less convincing window reflections.

For product photography and architectural visualization, FLUX.2 consistently delivered more accurate materials and lighting. SD 3.5 occasionally struggles with metallic surfaces and glass, producing results that look slightly "rendered" rather than photographed.

Winner: FLUX.2

FLUX.2's native 4MP resolution and superior material handling put it ahead for photorealism. SD 3.5 is close but not quite there.

2. Prompt Adherence: Does It Follow Instructions?

Prompt adherence — how accurately the model follows your text description — is the most underrated metric in image generation. A beautiful image that ignores half your prompt is useless for professional work.

We tested both models with a complex prompt: "Three different colored cats (orange, black, white) sitting on a wooden fence, sunset sky with purple clouds, a red barn visible in the distance, photorealistic style."

FLUX.2 handled this almost perfectly — three cats with correct colors, fence, sunset, barn. SD 3.5 got the general scene right but placed only two visible cats and made the barn red-brown rather than clearly red. In repeated tests, FLUX.2 was more reliable at counting objects and maintaining color assignments.

However, SD 3.5's advantage is its enormous ecosystem of fine-tuned models and LoRAs. If the base model doesn't follow your prompt well, you can almost certainly find a fine-tune that does — for faces, anime, architecture, or specific artists. FLUX.2's fine-tuning ecosystem is growing but hasn't caught up yet.

Winner: FLUX.2 (base model), SD 3.5 (with fine-tunes)

Out of the box, FLUX.2 follows prompts more reliably. With community fine-tunes, SD 3.5 can match or exceed it in specific domains.

3. Text Rendering: Can It Spell Words Correctly?

AI image generators have historically been terrible at rendering text. Signs, logos, and UI mockups would come out with garbled nonsense instead of actual letters. Both FLUX.2 and SD 3.5 have made significant strides here, but there's still a gap.

We tested with prompts like "A neon sign that says 'OPEN 24 HOURS' in a rainy city street at night" and "A coffee shop chalkboard menu with the words 'Today's Special: Lavender Latte $5.50' in elegant handwriting."

FLUX.2 rendered correct text in approximately 85% of our test cases. The neon sign test was nearly perfect — legible letters, correct spelling, appropriate glow effect. SD 3.5 got it right about 60% of the time, with occasional letter substitutions or missing characters.

If you're generating marketing materials, UI mockups, or anything requiring legible text, FLUX.2 is the clear choice. SD 3.5 has improved dramatically since 3.0 but still occasionally produces "AI gibberish" on complex text prompts.

Winner: FLUX.2

FLUX.2 currently has the best text rendering among open-source models. If text accuracy matters, pick FLUX.

4. Speed and VRAM: Can Your GPU Handle It?

This is where things get interesting — and where SD 3.5 fights back hard.

On an RTX 4090 (24GB), FLUX.2 generates a 2048×2048 image in approximately 8-12 seconds with FP16. SD 3.5 generates a 1024×1024 image in 3-5 seconds, but you can upscale with a refiner pass in about 8 seconds total for comparable resolution.

The real difference shows on mid-range GPUs. On an RTX 4070 (12GB):

  • FLUX.2: Requires FP8 quantization. Takes 25-40 seconds for a 1024×1024 image. 2048×2048 is possible but takes 60-90 seconds and may hit VRAM limits.
  • SD 3.5 Large: Runs comfortably in FP16 at 1024×1024. 4-7 seconds per image. Can batch process multiple images without running into VRAM issues.
  • SD 3.5 Medium: Even faster — 2-3 seconds at 1024×1024, runs on 8GB GPUs with ease.

5. Ecosystem and Flexibility

Stable Diffusion's biggest strength isn't the base model — it's everything built around it. SD 3.5 can use thousands of community-trained LoRAs (character styles, artistic styles, object concepts), hundreds of fine-tuned checkpoints, and a mature set of ControlNet models for precise composition control (pose, depth, edge detection, segmentation).

FLUX.2's ecosystem is growing fast. ComfyUI added FLUX.2 support within days of release. LoRA training tools have adapted to the DiT architecture, and the first wave of community LoRAs is impressive. But it will take months — possibly a year — to reach the depth of SD's ecosystem.

SD 3.5 also wins on interface diversity. You can use it with Auto1111 WebUI (beginner-friendly), ComfyUI (power users), Fooocus (simplicity-first), or InvokeAI (professional workflows). FLUX.2 works with ComfyUI, Forge, and SwarmUI, but the options are fewer.

6. The Verdict: Which One Should You Use?

🏆 Final Verdict

Choose FLUX.2 if:

  • You have a high-end GPU (16GB+ VRAM)
  • Photorealism and text rendering are your top priorities
  • You need the highest native resolution without upscaling tricks
  • You're doing commercial/professional work where quality is non-negotiable
  • You want to stay on the cutting edge and don't mind a smaller ecosystem

Choose Stable Diffusion 3.5 if:

  • You have a mid-range or consumer GPU (6-12GB VRAM)
  • You want the massive LoRA and fine-tune ecosystem
  • Speed and iteration matter more than absolute peak quality
  • You want multiple UI options (Auto1111, ComfyUI, Fooocus, InvokeAI)
  • You care about VRAM efficiency and batch processing

Bottom line: FLUX.2 is technically superior on image quality. SD 3.5 is more practical and versatile. For most users in mid-2026, the best setup is actually both — use FLUX.2 for final high-quality outputs and SD 3.5 for rapid iteration, experimentation, and specialized workflows via LoRAs.

Real-World Setup Recommendations

Budget Setup (~$0, 8-12GB GPU)

ComfyUI + Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium + a few key LoRAs. Fast, capable, and won't stress your hardware. Add FLUX.2 Schnell (distilled, faster version) for occasional high-quality renders.

Enthusiast Setup (~$0, 12-16GB GPU)

ComfyUI or SwarmUI + SD 3.5 Large as daily driver + FLUX.2 Dev with FP8 quantization for final renders. Best of both worlds.

Pro Setup (~$0, 24GB+ GPU)

SwarmUI managing both FLUX.2 (FP16) and SD 3.5 Large. Use FLUX.2 for client deliverables, SD 3.5 for exploration and LoRA-heavy workflows.

FAQ

Can I run FLUX.2 on an RTX 4070?

Yes, but with FP8 quantization and patience. Expect 25-40 seconds per image at 1024×1024. It works, but SD 3.5 will be a smoother experience on that card.

Is FLUX.2 really free?

The FLUX.2-dev model is released under Apache 2.0, so yes — free for both personal and commercial use. The "Pro" version is API-only and paid.

Which one has better NSFW/uncensored capabilities?

Both base models have safety filters, but SD 3.5's larger ecosystem means you can find community fine-tunes for almost any style or subject matter.

Can I use both models in the same workflow?

Yes. Tools like SwarmUI and ComfyUI support multiple backends. A common workflow: sketch and iterate with SD 3.5 (fast), then render the final version with FLUX.2 (high quality).
